# 1. Summary (Whiteboard Explanation)

## Background
An integral feature of realistic 3D graphics rendering in modern workloads is ray tracing (RT). Ray tracing, and more specifically to this work, path tracing (PT), is the practice of modeling rays as 3D vector that originate from the camera (or viewing point), flow through the pixels that compose a 2D image of the scene, and propagate into the scene. Typically, a single ray maps to a single pixel, and ray propagations bounce off object faces to determine a variety of shading properties, including illumination, shadows, reflections, and lighting effects. Ray bounces continue until one of three conditions: a preset, maximal number of bounces has been reached, a light source is hit, or a ray escapes the scene (occurs in open scenes, e.g. the sky). To accelerate the steps of the ray tracing algorithm, the Bounding Volume Hierarchy (BVH) Tree data structure is used to organize vertices and faces in the scene. Thus, to determine what objects rays bounce off of between a light source and the camera, the BVH tree is traversed via depth-first search. 

## CoopRT Design
Due to the importance for and complexity of support for ray tracing on GPU platforms, a dedicated hardware unit, called the RT unit, has become a standard inclusion. Despite the dedicated hardware support, there is still inefficiency in the ray tracing pipeline, namely, low utilization of parallel resources sourcing from the wide divergences of rays. This work presents CoopRT, a hardware modification to the RT unit that accelerates the BVH traversal in RT applications. A core component of CoopRT is an added hardware block called the Load Balancing Unit, which identifies pairs of threads where one thread is currently busy and has pending work and the other thread is idle. A thread is busy if the top of its BVH node traversal stack is non-empty. A thread has pending work if the rest of its BVH traversal stack is non-empty. A thread is idle if they meet any of the three, aforementioned, termination conditions. The LBU is fundamental in CoopRT's ability to exploit further parallelism by supporting work-stealing from busy threads to idle threads. Additional logic and structures are added to ensure functional correctness and necessary synchronization.

## Results and Success
The results of CoopRT are compelling. The authors evaluated 16 3D scenes from the Lumibench benchmark suite, whose BVH tree sizes ranged from 200KB to 1.7GB and whose tree depths ranged from 7 to 18. They evaluated whole algorithm performance, power, and energy changes seeing a geomean of 2.15x speedup, 2.02x increased power draw, and reduced energy consumption by 0.94x. Additionally, they supported the efficacy of CoopRT's feature of better exploiting parallelism through evaluations of thread utilization, instruction timelines, and tail-latencies, claiming an 94.6% increase in thread utilization. They further examined the impact on the changed memory access patterns, highlighting that CoopRT achieves 5.7x and 5.2x increased bandwidth utilization for L2 and DRAM, respectively. The authors provide an area evaluation from an RTL model of CoopRT and show that the changes are modest to existing RT unit designs. Finally, the authors also provide a scalability study, justifying the engineering decisions made in their hardware design.  

# 2. Key Insights
The key insight that explains CoopRT's success is the confluence of low SIMT efficiency of ray tracing workloads and the inherent parallelism of BVH traversal. The BVH trees often have a high degree of fan-out per node, 6 child nodes per parent node in this workload. Thus, when a thread encounters a node that passes an intersection test, all six children nodes are added to the parent nodes’ future traversal stack. Thus, the stack grows rapidly; at a faster rate than a single thread can process them. Thus, a single thread enqueues a sufficient amount of work for other threads to steal. This is only useful with the added feature of high divergence of path lengths from the threads themselves. Since ray bouncing is dependent on the incident angle of a ray with an object, the path of each ray is often unique. Since RT units employ SIMT-style execution, where threads execute in lockstep, this leads to a large disparity in thread-to-thread latency. This asymmetry leads to starvation of work for threads on short-paths, roughly 30.5% of threads. The combination of these two properties makes the core concept of CoopRT, work-stealing, an ideal improvement. 

# 3. Methodology Critique

## Strongest Aspect (Comprehensive)
The strongest aspect of the methodology is the sheer comprehensiveness of the evaluation. The authors present thorough characterization evaluations to justify their assumptions and identification of key insights, noted in figures 1, 2, 4, 10, and 11a. When presenting the impact of CoopRT, they cover a wide degree of axes: overall performance, power, and energy (fig 9), thread utilization (fig 10), instruction latency (fig 11), memory bandwidth (fig 12), sensitivity to warp buffer size (fig 13), tail latencies (fig 14), energy delay product (fig 15), L1 & L2 cache miss rates (fig 16), similar ray tracing workloads (fig 17), comparison to mobile GPU class systems (fig 18), RTL area analysis (table 3), and sensitivity study of CoopRT parameters (fig 19). Beyond a shadow of a doubt, they justified CoopRT's improvement over current methods.  

## Weakest Aspect (Depth)

At the cost of such a broad range of evaluation methodologies, the significance, and even some details necessary to determine the value of certain results are missing. Specifically, a deeper dive in the downstream effects of the changes to the memory access patterns. CoopRT does not introduce significantly more memory requests, but it does change the order the requests are issued. The authors note that there is an increase in L1 cache miss rates and assuage this concern via the overall improvement of performance. However, the authors also noted an increased power draw from CoopRT. It's unclear from this work, but the questions of whether the two are related and, if so, can they be mitigated naturally arise. 

# 4. Assumptions, Omissions, and Limitations

One thing that is unclear from this work is how CoopRT significantly differs from prior work. The authors explicitly cite prior works that have employed the same techniques in the SM cores of GPGPUs. Is this the novelty that this is a hardware-only solution, and thus a programmer need not make any software changes? If so, a comparison to a software only implementation of the mechanics of CoopRT would've been beneficial. The motivation that the solution to accelerate the BVH traversal in dedicated hardware structures and modifications to the RT unit is unclear without that comparison.

# 5. Broader Implications

While the focus of the paper is for accelerating PT in RT workloads, the same principles can be applied to the general domain of parallel graph algorithms. The authors explicitly note that regardless of breadth-first search or depth-first search mechanics and structures, CoopRT can be employed to exploit further parallelism. However, when mentioning this, they only reference other works that examine ray tracing workloads on GPUs. This work seems like it would be beneficial to a wide range of domains. One of particular interest might be sparse matrix operations, specifically LU-decomposition. The key features of having asymmetric work distribution coupled with a high level of exploitable parallelism makes it problem of the same class, and thus, potentially accelerated via CoopRT.
